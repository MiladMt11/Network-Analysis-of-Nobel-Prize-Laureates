'''Svetlana Alexandrovna Alexievich'''<ref>Her name is also transliterated as '''Aleksievich''' or '''Aleksiyevich'''. {{lang-be|Святла́на Алякса́ндраўна Алексіе́віч}} ''Svyatlana Alaksandrawna Aleksiyevich'' {{IPA-be|alʲɛksʲiˈjɛvʲit͡ʂ}}; {{lang-ru|link=no|Светла́на Алекса́ндровна Алексие́вич}} {{IPA-ru|ɐlʲɪksʲɪˈjevʲɪt͡ɕ}}; {{lang-uk|Світлана Олександрівна Алексієвич}}.</ref> (born 31 May 1948) is a Belarusian [[investigative journalist]], essayist and [[oral historian]] who writes in Russian. == Background ==
Born in the [[Western Ukraine|west Ukrainian]] town of [[Ivano-Frankivsk|Stanislav]] (Ivano-Frankivsk since 1962) to a Belarusian father and a Ukrainian mother,<ref>{{cite news|title=Remembering the Great Patriotic War was a political act|url=https://www.economist.com/news/books-and-arts/21725273-svetlana-alexievichs-unwomanly-face-war-was-part-peculiarly-soviet-debate|access-date=21 July 2017|work=[[The Economist]]|date=20 July 2017}}</ref> Svetlana Alexievich grew up in Belarus. After graduating from high school she worked as a reporter in several local newspapers. In 1972 she graduated from [[Belarusian State University]] and became a correspondent for the literary magazine ''Nyoman'' in [[Minsk]] (1976).<ref>[http://who.bdg.by/obj.php?&kod=262 Brief biography of Svetlana Alexievich (Russian)] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20140918014331/http://who.bdg.by/obj.php?&kod=262 |date=2014-09-18 }}, from ''Who is who in Belarus''</ref>

During her career in journalism, Alexievich specialized in crafting narratives based on witness testimonies. In the process, she wrote artfully constructed [[oral histories]]<ref name=pham/> of several dramatic events in Soviet history: the [[Second World War]],<ref name=":0">{{Cite web|url=http://www.ibtimes.com/4-books-read-svetlana-alexievitch-nobel-prize-literature-winner-ukrainian-belarusian-2132614|title=4 Books To Read By Svetlana Alexievitch, Nobel Prize Literature Winner And Ukrainian-Belarusian Author|website=[[International Business Times]]|date=2015-10-08|access-date=2016-08-16}}</ref>  [[Soviet–Afghan War|Afghan War]],<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.latimes.com/books/la-ca-jc-svetlana-alexievich-20151206-story.html|title=Svetlana Alexievich's 'Zinky Boys' gives voice to the voiceless|website=Los Angeles Times|date=3 December 2015|access-date=2016-08-16}}</ref> [[dissolution of the Soviet Union]],<ref name=":0" /> and the [[Chernobyl disaster]].<ref name=":0" /><ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.theguardian.com/books/2015/oct/08/svetlana-alexievich-wins-2015-nobel-prize-in-literature|title=Svetlana Alexievich wins 2015 Nobel prize in literature|last1=Flood |first1=Alison|date=2015-10-08|last2=Harding|first2=Luke|newspaper=The Guardian|language=en-GB|issn=0261-3077|last3=agencies|access-date=2016-08-16}}</ref>

In 1989 Alexievich's book ''Zinky Boys'' about the fallen soldiers, who had returned in zinc coffins from the [[Soviet-Afghan War]] of 1979 – 1985, was the subject of controversy, and she was accused of "defamation" and "desecration of the soldiers’ honor". Alexievich was tried a number of times between 1992 and 1996. After [[political persecution]] by the [[Alexander Lukashenko|Lukashenko]] administration,<ref>[http://www.lannan.org/bios/svetlana-alexievich Biography of Aleksievich] at Lannan Foundation website</ref> she left Belarus in 2000.<ref>{{cite web|title=Svetlana Alexievich: The Empire Will Not Pass Away Without Bloodshed|url=http://belarusians.co.uk/2014/09/18/svetlana-alexievich-the-empire-will-not-pass-away-without-bloodshed/|date=18 September 2014|website=www.belarusians.co.uk|access-date=8 October 2015|url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20150919065259/http://belarusians.co.uk/2014/09/18/svetlana-alexievich-the-empire-will-not-pass-away-without-bloodshed/|archive-date=19 September 2015}}</ref> The [[International Cities of Refuge Network]] offered her sanctuary and during the following decade she lived in Paris, Gothenburg and Berlin. In 2011, Alexievich moved back to Minsk.<ref name=PEN>{{cite web|title=Svetlana Alexievich|url=http://www.pen-deutschland.de/en/themen/writers-in-exile/ehemalige-stipendiaten/swetlana-alexijewitsch/|website=www.pen-deutschland.de|publisher=PEN-Zentrum Deutschland|accessdate=8 October 2015}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|title=Winners of the Peace Prize|url=http://www.friedenspreis-des-deutschen-buchhandels.de/445950/?aid=626480|website=www.friedenspreis-des-deutschen-buchhandels.de|publisher=[[Friedenspreis des Deutschen Buchhandels]]|accessdate=8 October 2015|language=German|archive-date=13 October 2015|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20151013205625/http://www.friedenspreis-des-deutschen-buchhandels.de/445950/?aid=626480|url-status=dead}}</ref>

== Literary work ==
Alexievich's books trace the emotional history of the [[Soviet Union|Soviet]] and post-Soviet individual through carefully constructed collages of interviews.<ref name=Alter8Oct>{{cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2015/10/09/books/svetlana-alexievich-nobel-prize-literature.html|date=8 October 2015|access-date=8 October 2015|title=Svetlana Alexievich Wins Nobel Prize in Literature|work=The New York Times|first=Alexandra|last=Alter}}</ref> According to Russian writer and critic [[Dmitry Bykov]], her books owe much to the ideas of Belarusian writer [[Ales Adamovich]], who felt that the best way to describe the horrors of the 20th century was not by creating fiction but through recording the testimonies of witnesses.<ref name=bykov.echo>{{cite web|last1=Быков|first1=Дмитрий|script-title=ru:О присуждении Светлане Алексиевич Нобелевской премии по литературе|url=http://echo.msk.ru/programs/beseda/1636786-echo/|publisher=[[Echo of Moscow]]|access-date=8 October 2015|language=ru}}</ref> Belarusian poet [[Uladzimir Nyaklyayew]] called Adamovich "her literary godfather". He also named the documentary novel ''I'm From Fire Village'' ({{lang-be|Я з вогненнай вёскі}}) by Ales Adamovich, [[Janka Bryl]] and Uladzimir Kalesnik, about the villages burned by the [[Germans|German]] troops during the [[German occupation of Belarus during World War II|occupation of Belarus]], as the main single book that has influenced Alexievich's attitude to literature.<ref>{{cite web|title=Някляеў: Шанцы Беларусі на Нобелеўскую прэмію як ніколі высокія|url=http://nn.by/?c=ar&i=116304|publisher=[[Nasha Niva]]|access-date=8 October 2015|language=be}} Original quote: "Калі ўся руская літаратура выйшла, як сцвярджаў Дастаеўскі, з «Шыняля» Гогаля, то ўся творчасць Алексіевіч – з дакументальнай кнігі Алеся Адамовіча, Янкі Брыля і Уладзіміра Калесніка «Я з вогненнай вёскі». Адамовіч — яе літаратурны хросны". Rough translation: "If the entire Russian literature came, as [[Fyodor Dostoyevsky|Dostoyevsky]] stated, from the [[Nikolai Gogol|Gogol]]'s ''Overcoat'', then the entire writings of Alexievich came from the documentary book of Ales Adamovich, Yanka Bryl and Uladzimir Kalesnik ''I'm from the flamy village''. Adamovich is her literary godfather".</ref> Alexievich has confirmed the influence of Adamovich and Belarusian writer [[Vasil Bykaŭ]], among others.<ref>{{cite web|title=Svetlana Alexievich: It is not my victory alone, but also a victory of our culture and the country|url=http://eng.belta.by/culture/view/svetlana-alexievich-it-is-not-my-victory-alone-but-also-a-victory-of-our-culture-and-the-country-86012-2015/|publisher=[[Belarusian Telegraph Agency]]|access-date=8 October 2015|date=2015-10-08}}</ref> She regards [[Varlam Shalamov]] as the best writer of the 20th century.<ref name=BBC151224>{{cite news |url=http://www.bbc.com/russian/society/2015/12/151224_alexievich_interview_newshour |script-title=ru:Алексиевич: "Есть мой путь, и я иду этим путём" |language=ru |trans-title=Alexievich interview |work=BBC News |date=24 December 2015}}</ref>

Her most notable works in English translation include a collection of first-hand accounts from [[Soviet–Afghan War|the war in Afghanistan]] (''Zinky Boys: Soviet Voices from a Forgotten War'')<ref name=moscow /> and an oral history of the Chernobyl disaster (''Chernobyl Prayer / Voices from Chernobyl'').<ref>{{cite web|title=Voices From Chernobyl|url=http://www.fairewinds.org/nuclear-energy-education/voices-from-chernobyl|publisher=Fairewinds Education|access-date=8 October 2015}}</ref> Alexievich describes the theme of her works this way:

{{quote|If you look back at the whole of our history, both Soviet and post-Soviet, it is a huge common grave and a blood bath. An eternal dialog of the executioners and the victims. The accursed Russian questions: what is to be done and who is to blame. The revolution, the gulags, the Second World War, the Soviet–Afghan war hidden from the people, the downfall of the great empire, the downfall of the giant socialist land, the land-utopia, and now a challenge of cosmic dimensions – Chernobyl. This is a challenge for all the living things on earth. Such is our history. And this is the theme of my books, this is my path, my circles of hell, from man to man.<ref name=Svetlana>{{cite web|title=THE CHRONICLER OF THE UTOPIAN LAND|url=http://www.alexievich.info/biogr_EN.html|website=www.alexievich.info|publisher=Svetlana Alexievich|access-date=8 October 2015}}</ref>}}

Her first book, ''War's Unwomanly Face'', came out in 1985. It was repeatedly reprinted and sold more than two million copies.<ref name=moscow>{{cite web|last1=Osipovich|first1=Alexander|title=True Stories|url=http://www.themoscowtimes.com/arts_n_ideas/article/true-stories/365300.html|website=[[The Moscow Times]]|date=19 March 2004|access-date=8 October 2015}}</ref> The book was finished in 1983 and published (in short edition) in [[Oktyabr (magazine)|''Oktyabr'']], a Soviet monthly literary magazine, in February 1984.<ref>С. Алексиевич. ''У войны — не женское лицо''. Октябрь, 1984(2). (S. Alexievich. ''War's Unwomanly Face''. [[Oktyabr (magazine)|Oktyabr]], 1984(2).)</ref> In 1985, the book was published by several publishers, and the number of printed copies reached 2,000,000 in the next five years.<ref name=tut>{{cite web|last1=Карпов|first1=Евгений|script-title=ru:Светлана Алексиевич получила Нобелевскую премию по литературе – первую в истории Беларуси|url=http://news.tut.by/culture/467702.html|website=www.tut.by|publisher=[[Tut.By]]|date=8 October 2015|access-date=8 October 2015|language=ru}} Quote: "Первая книга — «У войны не женское лицо» — была готова в 1983 и пролежала в издательстве два года. Автора обвиняли в пацифизме, натурализме и развенчании героического образа советской женщины. «Перестройка» дала благотворный толчок."</ref> This novel is made up of [[monologue]]s of women in the war speaking about the aspects of World War II that had never been related before.<ref name=moscow /> Another book, ''The Last Witnesses: the Book of Unchildlike Stories'', describes personal memories of children during wartime. The war seen through women's and children's eyes revealed a new world of feelings.<ref name=minsk>{{cite web|last1=Golesnik|first1=Sergey|title=Black-and-white war monologues stir hearts|url=http://www.sb.by/files/MT/09/N26/10.pdf|website=www.sb.by|publisher=The Minsk Times|date=16 July 2009|access-date=8 October 2015}}</ref> 

In 1992, Alexievich published "Boys in Zinc". The course of the Soviet-Afghan War (1979-1989) is told through emotive personal testimony from unnamed participants of the war; from nurses, to commissioned officers and pilots, mothers and widows. Each provides an excerpt of the Soviet-Afghan War which was disguised in the face of criticism first as political support, then intervention, and finally humanitarian aid to the Afghan people. Alexievich writes at the beginning of the book: 
{{quote|After the great wars of the twentieth century and the mass deaths, writing about the modern (small) wars, like the war in Afghanistan, requires different ethical and metaphysical stances. What must be reclaimed is the small, the personal, and the specific. The single human being. The only human being for someone, not as the state regards him, but who he is for his mother, for his wife, for his child. How can we recover a normal vision of life?<ref> Alexievich, "Boys in Zinc" p.19</ref>}}
Alexievich was not embedded with the Red Army due to her reputation in the Soviet Union, instead she travelled to Kabul on her own prerogative during the war, and gathered many accounts from veterans returned from Afghanistan. In "Boys in Zinc", Alexievich calls herself 'a historian of the untraceable' and 'strive[s] desperately (from book to book) to do one thing - reduce history to the human being.'<ref> Alexievich, "Boys in Zinc" p.18</ref> She brings brutally honest accounts of the war to lay at the feet of the Soviet people, but claims no heroism for herself: 'I went [to watch them assemble pieces of boys blown up by an anti-tank mine] and there was nothing heroic about it, because I fainted there. Perhaps it was from the heat, perhaps from the shock. I want to be honest.'<ref> Alexievich, "Boys in Zinc", p. 20</ref> The monologues which make up the book are honest (if edited for clarity) reproductions of the oral histories Alexievich collected, including those who perhaps did not understand her purpose: 'What's your book for? Who's it for? None of us who came back from there will like it anyway. How can you possibly tell people how it was? The dead camels and dead men lying in a single pool of blood, with their blood mingled together. Who wants that?'<ref>Alexievich, "Boys in Zinc" p.30</ref> Alexievich was brought to trial in Minsk between 1992 and 1996, accused of distorting and falsifying the testimony of Afghan veterans and their mothers who were 'offended [...] that their boys were portrayed exclusively as soulless killer-robots, pillagers, drug addicts and rapists...' <ref>Griegoriev. "Vecherny Minsk", 2 June 1992</ref> The trial, while apparently defending the honour of the army and veterans, is widely seen as an attempt to preserve old ideology in post-communist Belarus. The Belarus League for Human Rights claims that in the early 1990s multiple cases were directed against democratically inclined intelligentsia with politically motivated verdicts. <ref>The Belarus League for Human Rights cited in the Epilogue of "Boys in Zinc"</ref>

In 1993, she published ''Enchanted with Death'', a book about attempted and completed suicides due to the downfall of the Soviet Union. Many people felt inseparable from the [[Communist ideology]] and unable to accept the new order surely and the newly interpreted history.<ref>{{cite web|last1=Saxena|first1=Ranjana|title=On Reading 'Enchanted with Death' by Svetlana Aleksievich: Narratives of Nostalgia and Loss|url=http://c-linkage.com/abs/iccees2015/program/abstractview.php?uid=1107&pid=3173&role=3|publisher=2013 [[International Council for Central and East European Studies|ICCEES]] IX World Congress|access-date=8 October 2015|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160304121030/http://c-linkage.com/abs/iccees2015/program/abstractview.php?uid=1107&pid=3173&role=3|archive-date=4 March 2016|url-status=dead|df=dmy-all}}</ref>

Her books were not published by Belarusian state-owned publishing houses after 1993, while private publishers in Belarus have only published two of her books: ''Chernobyl Prayer'' in 1999  and ''Second-hand Time'' in 2013, both translated into Belarusian.<ref>{{cite web|script-title=ru:Госиздательства Беларуси не выпускали книги Алексиевич больше 20 лет|url=http://news.tut.by/society/467800.html|website=www.tut.by|publisher=[[Tut.By]]|date=8 October 2015|access-date=8 October 2015|language=ru}}</ref> As a result, Alexievich has been better known in the rest of world than in Belarus.<ref>{{cite magazine|script-title=ru:Впервые за долгое время премия вручается автору в жанре нон-фикшн|url=http://www.kommersant.ru/Doc/2827474|magazine=[[Kommersant]]|date=8 October 2015|access-date=8 October 2015|language=ru}} Quote: "Но она известно гораздо больше за пределами Белоруссии, чем в Белоруссии. Она уважаемый европейский писатель".</ref>

She has been described as the first [[journalist]] to receive the [[Nobel Prize in Literature]].<ref>[https://www.bbc.com/news/entertainment-arts-34475251 Svetlana Alexievich wins Nobel Literature prize], by [[BBC]]</ref> She herself rejects the notion that she is a journalist, and, in fact, Alexievich’s chosen genre is sometimes called "documentary literature": an artistic rendering of real events, with a degree of poetic license.<ref name=pham>{{cite news|url=https://newrepublic.com/article/135719/witness-tampering|title=Witness Tampering|last=Pinkham|first=Sophie|date=29 August 2016|work=[[The New Republic]]|access-date=30 August 2016}}</ref> In her own words:
{{quote|I’ve been searching for a literary method that would allow the closest possible approximation to real life. Reality has always attracted me like a magnet, it tortured and hypnotized me, I wanted to capture it on paper. So I immediately appropriated this genre of actual human voices and confessions, witness evidences and documents. This is how I hear and see the world – as a chorus of individual voices and a collage of everyday details. This is how my eye and ear function. In this way all my mental and emotional potential is realized to the full. In this way I can be simultaneously a writer, reporter, sociologist, psychologist and preacher.}}

26 October 2019 Alexievich was elected chairman of the [[Belarusian PEN Center]].<ref>[https://euroradio.fm/pen-geyt-vyartanne-bludnaga-sevyarynca-nasuperak-kulturnym-marksistam ПЭН-ГЕЙт: вяртанне блуднага Севярынца насуперак "культурным марксістам"]</ref>

==Political activity==
During the [[2020 Belarusian protests]] Alexievich became a member of the [[Coordination Council (Belarus)|Coordination Council]] of [[Sviatlana Tsikhanouskaya]].<ref>{{cite web|url=https://rada.vision/chleny-soveta|title=Основной состав Координационного Совета|website=rada.vision|access-date=November 28, 2020}}</ref>

On 20 August, Alexander Konyuk, the [[Prosecutor-General]] of Belarus, initiated criminal proceedings against the members of the Coordination Council under Article 361 of the [[Criminal Code (Belarus)|Belarusian Criminal Code]], on the grounds of attempting to seize state power and harming national security.<ref name=tme>{{cite web|url=https://t.me/mkbelarus/9106|title=МАЯ КРАІНА БЕЛАРУСЬ|website=Telegram}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.urdupoint.com/en/world/belarus-opens-criminal-probe-against-oppositi-1006745.html|title=Belarus Opens Criminal Probe Against Oppositions Coordination Council- Prosecutor General|website=UrduPoint}}</ref>

On 26 August, Alexievich was questioned by Belarusian authorities about her involvement in the council.<ref>{{Cite news|last1=Central|first1=Shaun Walker|last2=correspondent|first2=eastern Europe|date=2020-08-26|title=Belarusian Nobel winner questioned over opposition council|language=en-GB|work=The Guardian|url=https://www.theguardian.com/world/2020/aug/26/belarusian-nobel-winner-questioned-over-opposition-council|access-date=2020-08-27|issn=0261-3077}}</ref>

On 9 September 2020, Alexievich alerted the press that "men in black masks" were trying to enter her apartment in central [[Minsk]]. "I have no friends and companions left in the Coordinating Council. All are in prison or have been forcibly sent into exile," she wrote in a statement. "First they kidnapped the country; now it's the turn of the best among us. But hundreds more will replace those who have been torn from our ranks. It is not the Coordinating Council that has rebelled. It is the country."<ref>{{Cite news|last=Gatinois|first=Claire|date=9 September 2020|title=Svetlana Alexievitch, Prix Nobel de littérature, à son tour menacée par le régime en Biélorussie|work=Le Monde|url=https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Svetlana_Alexievich?action=edit|access-date=9 September 2020}}</ref> Diplomats from Lithuania, Poland, the Czech Republic, Romania, Slovakia, and Sweden began to keep a round-the-clock watch on Alexievich's home to prevent her abduction by security services.<ref>https://euobserver.com/foreign/149386</ref><ref>https://www.kob.com/news/another-leading-opposition-activist-detained-in-belarus/5856349/?cat=602</ref>

On 28 September 2020, Alexievich left Belarus for Germany, promising to return depending on political conditions in Belarus. Prior to her departure, she was the last member of the Coordination Council who was not in exile or under arrest.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.dw.com/en/svetlana-alexievich-nobel-laureate-leaves-belarus-for-germany/a-55079649|title=Svetlana Alexievich: Nobel laureate leaves Belarus for Germany|work=Deutsche Welle|access-date=November 28, 2020}}</ref>

In August 2021, her book ''The Last Witnesses'' was excluded from the school curriculum in Belarus and her name was removed from the curriculum.<ref>[https://belaruspartisan.by/bel/life/542825/ З праграмы літаратуры для беларускіх школаў выкрасьлілі кнігу Алексіевіч]</ref><ref>[https://novayagazeta.ru/articles/2021/08/08/iz-belorusskoi-shkolnoi-programmy-po-literature-iskliuchili-proizvedeniia-solzhenitsyna-nabokova-i-aleksievich-news Из белорусской школьной программы по литературе исключили произведения Солженицына, Набокова и Алексиевич]</ref> It was assumed that the exclusion was made for her political activity.<ref>[https://gazetaby.com/post/vazhno-ponimat-kto-ostanetsya-v-istorii-velikim-a-/178536/ «Важно понимать, кто останется в истории великим, а у кого в Википедии укажут цифры политзаключенных»]</ref>

== Awards and honours ==

Alexievich is a member of the advisory committee of the [[Lettre Ulysses Award]]. She will give the inaugural [[Anna Politkovskaya]] Memorial Lecture at the [[British Library]] on 9 October 2019.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.bl.uk/events/svetlana-alexievich-the-anna-politkovskaya-memorial-lecture|title=Svetlana Alexievich: The Anna Politkovskaya Memorial Lecture|website=The British Library|access-date=2019-10-07}}</ref> The lecture is an international platform to amplify the voices of women journalists and human rights defenders working in war and conflict zones.
