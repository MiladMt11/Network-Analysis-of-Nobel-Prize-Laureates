'''Albert Fert''' ({{IPA-fr|albɛʁ fɛʁ|lang}}; born 7 March 1938, [[Carcassonne]], [[France]]) is a [[French people|French]] [[physics|physicist]] and one of the discoverers of [[giant magnetoresistance]] which brought about a breakthrough in [[gigabyte]] [[hard disks]]. Currently, he is an emeritus professor at [[Paris-Saclay University]] in [[Orsay]], scientific director of a joint laboratory (''Unité mixte de recherche'') between the [[Centre national de la recherche scientifique]] (National Scientific Research Centre) and [[Thales Group]], and adjunct professor at [[Michigan State University]]. He was awarded the 2007 [[Nobel Prize in Physics]] together with [[Peter Grünberg]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://nobelprize.org/nobel_prizes/physics/laureates/2007/press.html|title=The Nobel Prize in Physics 2007}}</ref>

==Biography==
Fert graduated in 1962 from the [[École Normale Supérieure]] in [[Paris]].<ref name=":1">{{Cite web|title=Prof. Albert Fert {{!}} GSE Mainz|url=https://www.mainz.uni-mainz.de/prof-albert-fert/|access-date=2021-01-19|website=www.mainz.uni-mainz.de}}</ref> There he followed the courses of great physicists like [[Alfred Kastler]] or [[Jacques Friedel]], and was passionate about photography and cinema (he was a big admirer of the work of [[Ingmar Bergman]]).<ref>{{Cite web|title=Albert Fert, un Nobel amoureux de Bergman|url=https://www.lefigaro.fr/sciences/2007/12/31/01008-20071231ARTFIG00196-albert-fert-un-nobel-amoureux-de-bergman.php|access-date=2021-01-19|website=LEFIGARO|language=fr}}</ref>

After graduating from the École Normale Supérieure, he attended the [[University of Grenoble]] and in 1963 received his Ph.D. (''doctorat de troisième cycle'') from the [[University of Paris]] with a thesis prepared in the fundamental electronic [[Paris-Saclay Faculty of Sciences|Orsay Faculty of Sciences]] and in the physical spectrometry laboratory of the University of Grenoble Faculty of Sciences.

After his return from military service in 1965, he was assistant professor at the Orsay Faculty of Sciences of the [[University of Paris XI]] ([[Université Paris-Sud]]),<ref>{{Cite web|last=|first=|date=|title=The Nobel Prize in Physics 2007 – Albert Fert – Facts|url=https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/physics/2007/fert/facts/|url-status=live|archive-url=|archive-date=|access-date=2021-01-19|website=NobelPrize.org|language=en-US}}</ref> and prepared under the direction of Ian Campbell within the [[Laboratoire de Physique des Solides|Laboratory of Solid Physics]] of the faculty for a doctorate Sc.D. (''doctorat des sciences'') in Physical Sciences devoted to the properties of electrical transport in [[nickel]] and [[iron]], which he completed in 1970,<ref name=":1" /> and was made a professor there in 1976.

He worked as research director for the university's [[Condensed matter physics|condensed-matter physics]] laboratory (1970–1995) prior to heading to Unité Mixte de Physique, a laboratory jointly run by the Université Paris-Sud and the technology company [[Thales Group|Thales]].

In 1988, Albert Fert at Orsay in France and Peter Gruenberg in Jülich in Germany, simultaneously and independently, discovered the [[giant magnetoresistance]] (GMR) of the magnetic multilayers.<ref>{{Cite journal|last1=Baibich|first1=M. N.|last2=Broto|first2=J. M.|last3=Fert|first3=A.|last4=Van Dau|first4=F. Nguyen|last5=Petroff|first5=F.|last6=Etienne|first6=P.|last7=Creuzet|first7=G.|last8=Friederich|first8=A.|last9=Chazelas|first9=J.|date=1988-11-21|title=Giant Magnetoresistance of (001)Fe/(001)Cr Magnetic Superlattices|journal=Physical Review Letters|volume=61|issue=21|pages=2472–2475|doi=10.1103/PhysRevLett.61.2472|pmid=10039127|bibcode=1988PhRvL..61.2472B|url=http://www.lume.ufrgs.br/bitstream/10183/99075/1/000014840.pdf|doi-access=free}}</ref><ref>{{Cite journal|last1=Binasch|first1=G.|last2=Grünberg|first2=P.|last3=Saurenbach|first3=F.|last4=Zinn|first4=W.|date=1989-03-01|title=Enhanced magnetoresistance in layered magnetic structures with antiferromagnetic interlayer exchange|journal=Physical Review B|volume=39|issue=7|pages=4828–4830|doi=10.1103/PhysRevB.39.4828|pmid=9948867|bibcode=1989PhRvB..39.4828B|doi-access=free}}</ref> This discovery is recognized as the birth of spintronics,<ref>{{Cite book|title=Handbook of spin transport and magnetism|date=2012|publisher=CRC Press|others=Tsymbal, E. Y. (Evgeny Y.), Zutic, Igor.|isbn=9781439803776|location=Boca Raton, Florida|oclc=756724063}}</ref><ref name=":0">{{Cite journal|last1=Chappert|first1=Claude|last2=Fert|first2=Albert|last3=Dau|first3=Frédéric Nguyen Van|title=The emergence of spin electronics in data storage|journal=Nature Materials|language=En|volume=6|issue=11|pages=813–823|doi=10.1038/nmat2024|pmid=17972936|issn=1476-4660|bibcode=2007NatMa...6..813C|year=2007}}</ref> a research field which is often described as a new type of electronics exploiting not only the electric charge of the electrons but also their magnetism (their spin). Spintronics has already important applications. One knows that the introduction of GMR read heads in hard disks has led to a considerable increase of their capacity of information storage.<ref name=":0" /> Other spintronic properties are exploited in the M-RAM<ref name=":0" /><ref>{{Cite journal|last=Åkerman|first=Johan|date=2005-04-22|title=Toward a Universal Memory|journal=Science|language=en|volume=308|issue=5721|pages=508–510|doi=10.1126/science.1110549|issn=0036-8075|pmid=15845842|s2cid=60577959}}</ref> that are expected to impact soon the technology of the computers and phones. In 2007, together with Prof. Grünberg, they received the renowned [[Japan Prize|Japan Award]] (300.000 Euro) for their discovery of GMR. The same year, they received the Nobel prize in Physics.

In October 2006, Professor Fert received the honorary doctorate from the Department of Physics of the [[Technical University of Kaiserslautern|University of Kaiserslautern]].<ref name=":1" />

Albert Fert had many contributions to the development of spintronics and, after his 2007 Nobel Prize, he is exploring the emerging direction of the exploitation of topological properties in spintronics.<ref>{{Cite journal|last1=Soumyanarayanan|first1=Anjan|last2=Reyren|first2=Nicolas|last3=Fert|first3=Albert|last4=Panagopoulos|first4=Christos|date=2016-11-23|title=Emergent phenomena induced by spin–orbit coupling at surfaces and interfaces|journal=Nature|language=En|volume=539|issue=7630|pages=509–517|doi=10.1038/nature19820|pmid=27882972|issn=1476-4687|arxiv=1611.09521|bibcode=2016arXiv161109521S|s2cid=4452338}}</ref> His most recent works are on the topologically protected magnetic solitons called skyrmions<ref>{{Cite journal|last1=Fert|first1=Albert|last2=Reyren|first2=Nicolas|last3=Cros|first3=Vincent|title=Magnetic skyrmions: advances in physics and potential applications|journal=Nature Reviews Materials|language=En|volume=2|issue=7|pages=17031|doi=10.1038/natrevmats.2017.31|issn=2058-8437|arxiv=1712.07236|bibcode=2017NatRM...217031F|year=2017}}</ref> and on the conversion between charge and spin current by topological insulators.<ref>{{Cite journal|last1=Rojas-Sánchez|first1=J.-C.|last2=Oyarzún|first2=S.|last3=Fu|first3=Y.|last4=Marty|first4=A.|last5=Vergnaud|first5=C.|last6=Gambarelli|first6=S.|last7=Vila|first7=L.|last8=Jamet|first8=M.|last9=Ohtsubo|first9=Y.|date=2016-03-01|title=Spin to Charge Conversion at Room Temperature by Spin Pumping into a New Type of Topological Insulator: $\ensuremath{\alpha}$-Sn Films|journal=Physical Review Letters|volume=116|issue=9|pages=096602|doi=10.1103/PhysRevLett.116.096602|pmid=26991190|arxiv=1509.02973|bibcode=2016PhRvL.116i6602R}}</ref>
