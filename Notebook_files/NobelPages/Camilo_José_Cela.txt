'''Camilo José Cela y Trulock, 1st Marquis of Iria Flavia''' ({{IPA-es|kamilo xoˈse ˈθela|lang}}; 11 May 1916 – 17 January 2002) was a [[Spaniards|Spanish]] novelist, poet, story writer and essayist associated with the [[Generation of '36]] movement.

He was awarded the 1989 [[Nobel Prize in Literature]] "for a rich and intensive prose, which with restrained compassion forms a challenging vision of man's vulnerability".<ref>{{cite web|url=http://nobelprize.org/nobel_prizes/literature/laureates/1989/index.html|title=Nobel Prize in Literature 1989|publisher=[[Nobel Foundation]]|access-date=2008-10-17}}</ref>

==Childhood and early career==
Camilo José Cela was born in the rural parish of [[Iria Flavia]], in [[Padrón]], [[Province of A Coruña|A Coruña]], Spain, on 11 May 1916.<ref>{{Cite web |url=https://www.nobelprize.org/nobel_prizes/literature/laureates/1989/cela-bio.html |title=Camilo José Cela - Biographical |website=www.nobelprize.org|access-date=2016-07-13}}</ref> He was the oldest child of nine.<ref name=":0">{{Cite web |url=https://www.theguardian.com/news/2002/jan/18/guardianobituaries.books |title=Obituary: Camilo José Cela |last=Eaude |first=Michael |date=2002-01-18 |website=The Guardian |access-date=2016-07-13}}</ref> His father, Camilo Crisanto Cela y Fernández, was [[Galician people|Galician]]. His mother, Camila Emanuela Trulock y Bertorini, was a Galician of [[English people|English]] and [[Italians|Italian]] ancestry. The family was upper-middle-class and Cela described his childhood as being "so happy it was hard to grow up."<ref name=":0" />

He lived with his family in [[Vigo]] from 1921 to 1925, when they moved to [[Madrid]]. There, Cela studied at a [[Piarist]] school. In 1931 he was diagnosed with [[tuberculosis]] and admitted to the [[sanatorium]] of [[Guadarrama]], where he took advantage of his free time to work on his novel ''Pabellón de reposo''. While recovering from the illness Cela began intensively reading works by [[José Ortega y Gasset]] and [[Antonio de Solís y Ribadeneyra]].

The [[Spanish Civil War]] broke out in 1936 when Cela was 20 years old and just recovering from his illness. His political leanings were conservative and he escaped to the rebel zone. He enlisted himself as a soldier but was wounded and hospitalized in [[Logroño]].

==Career==
The civil war ended in 1939; after the war, Cela became indecisive towards his university studies and ended up working in a bureau of textile industries. It was here where he began to write what would become his first novel, {{lang|es|La familia de Pascual Duarte}} (''[[The Family of Pascual Duarte]]''), which was finally published when he was 26, in 1942. Pascual Duarte has trouble finding validity in conventional morality and commits a number of crimes, including murders, for which he feels nothing. The novel is of particular importance as it played a large part in shaping the direction of the post-[[World War II]] Spanish novel.{{citation needed|date=December 2020}} [[Image:Ruizanglada - 1988 Camilo José Cela recibe una obra de Ruizanglada en el homenaje de la Peña Solera Aragonesa Little.jpg|thumb|left|Camilo José Cela (right) in 1988.]]

Cela became a censor in [[Francoist Spain]] in 1943.<ref>[http://www.eldiario.es/cultura/libros/censor-censurado-Camilo-Jose-Cela_0_514449689.html ''El censor censurado; cien añ0s de Camilo José Cela'']</ref> Perhaps his best-known work was produced during a period where his own writing came under scrutiny from his fellow censors, including {{lang|es|[[The Hive (Cela novel)|La colmena]]}} (''The Hive'') which was published in Buenos Aires in 1951, having been banned in Spain.<ref>[https://www.themodernnovel.org/europe/w-europe/spain/cela/colmena/ ''The modern novel: Camilo José Cela: La colmena (The hive)'']</ref> The novel features more than 300 characters and a style showing the influence of both Spanish [[Literary realism|realism]]  and contemporary English and French-language authors. Cela's signature style&mdash;a sarcastic, often grotesque, form of realism&mdash;is epitomized in {{lang|es|La colmena}}.

From the late 1960s, with the publication of {{lang|es|San Camilo 1936}}, Cela's work became increasingly experimental. In 1988 he wrote {{lang|es|Cristo versus Arizona}} (''Christ versus Arizona''), which tells the story of the [[Gunfight at the O.K. Corral]] in a single sentence that is more than one hundred pages long.

==Legacy==
On 26 May 1957, Cela was appointed a member of the [[Royal Spanish Academy]] and given Seat Q. He was appointed Royal Senator in the [[Constituent Cortes]], where he exerted some influence in the wording of the [[Spanish Constitution of 1978]]. In 1987, he was awarded the [[Prince of Asturias Award for Literature]].

Cela was awarded the [[Nobel Prize in Literature]] in 1989 ''"for a rich and intensive prose, which with restrained compassion forms a challenging vision of man's vulnerability"''.<ref>[http://nobelprize.org/nobel_prizes/literature/laureates/1989/index.html Nobel prize citation]</ref>

In 1994, he was awarded the [[Premio Planeta]],<ref>[http://www.planetadelibros.com/camilo-jose-cela-autor-000000179.html Camilo José Cela]</ref> although some question the objectivity of the awards, and winners on occasion have refused to accept it.{{Citation needed|date=August 2008}} Two years later, in recognition of his contributions to literature, Cela was ennobled on 17 May 1996 by [[Juan Carlos I of Spain|King Juan Carlos I]], who gave Cela the hereditary title of [[Marquess of Iria Flavia]] in the [[Spanish nobility|nobility of Spain]]. On his death the title passed to his son [[Camilo José Cela Conde]].

[[File:Coat of Arms of the 1st Marquis of Iria Flavia.svg|right|thumb|250px|Cela's arms as 1st [[Marqués de Iria Flavia|Marquess of Iria Flavia]] (1996)]]

==Controversies==
''The Hive'' was first published in Argentina, as [[Francisco Franco|Franco]]'s [[Spanish State]] banned it because of the perceived immorality of its content referencing erotic themes. This meant that his name could no longer appear in the printed media.<ref>[http://www.theparisreview.org/interviews/1396/the-art-of-fiction-no-145-camilo-jos-cela The Paris Review]</ref> Nevertheless, Cela remained loyal to [[Francoist Spain]], even working as an informer for the Spanish secret police by reporting on the activities of dissident groups<ref>[http://www.counterpunch.org/navarro10302004.html Counterpunch] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20100822073636/http://www.counterpunch.org/navarro10302004.html |date=2010-08-22 }}</ref><ref>Unearthing Franco's Legacy, p.15 University of Notre Dame Press, {{ISBN|0-268-03268-8}}</ref> and betraying fellow intellectuals.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.theguardian.com/world/2004/sep/25/books.spain|website=[[The Guardian]]|date=25 September 2004|title=Spanish novelist spied for Franco's regime}}</ref>

In his later years Cela became known for his scandalous outbursts; in an interview with [[Mercedes Milá]] for Spanish state television he boasted of his ability to absorb litres of water via his anus while offering to demonstrate.<ref name="La Voz">[http://www.lavozdegalicia.es/especiales/camilo/noticia.jsp?TEXTO=100000010438 {{lang|es|Todos los títulos fueron suyos}}] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20071007124013/http://www.lavozdegalicia.es/especiales/camilo/noticia.jsp?TEXTO=100000010438 |date=2007-10-07 }}, Luis Ventoso, {{lang|es|[[La Voz de Galicia]]}}, 18 January 2002.</ref> Cela had already scandalized Spanish society with his {{lang|es|Diccionario secreto}} (Secret Dictionary, 1969–1971), a dictionary of slang and taboo words.

Cela described{{when|date=December 2020}} the Spanish [[Premio Cervantes|Cervantes Prize]] for lifetime achievement as a writer as being "covered with shit".<ref name="El Mundo">[http://www.elmundo.es/2002/01/18/cultura/400804.html {{lang|es|La leyenda del gran provocador}}], Ángel Vivas, {{lang|es|[[El Mundo (Spain)|El Mundo]]}}, 18 January 2002.</ref> In 1995 he was offered the prize, which he accepted.

In 1998, Cela expressed discomfort towards the presence of [[Human male sexuality#Homosexuality|homosexual]] groups at the commemoration of [[Federico García Lorca]]'s centenary, stating that, "For me, I would prefer a more straightforward and less anecdotal commemoration without the support of gay groups. I have nothing against gays, I just do not take it up the ass".<ref>[http://www.libertaddigital.com/fotos/camilo-jose-cela-polemicas-frases-cultura-1008999/federico-garcia-lorca.jpg.html Artículo homófobo sobre Lorca]</ref>

==Death==
Cela died from [[heart disease]] on 17 January 2002 at the Hospital Centro in [[Madrid]], aged 85. He was buried in his hometown at the parish cemetery of ''Santa María de Adina''.<ref>[http://news.bbc.co.uk/1/hi/entertainment/arts/1765469.stm BBC obituary]</ref>

Cela's will was contested because he favoured his widow and second younger wife, Marina Castaño, over his son [[Camilo José Cela Conde]] from his first marriage to Rosario Conde.<ref>[http://www.libertaddigital.com/fotos/camilo-jose-cela-polemicas-frases-cultura-1008999/camilo-hijo.jpg.html Viuda e hijo, enfrentados por su herencia]</ref>
