'''David James Thouless''' {{post-nominals|country=GBR|size=100%|FRS}}<ref name=frs/> ({{IPAc-en|ˈ|θ|aʊ|l|ɛ|s}}; 21 September 1934 – 6 April 2019<ref name=whoswho/><ref name=Obit/><ref name="nobelfacts"/>) was a British [[Condensed matter physics|condensed-matter]] [[physicist]].<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.lanl.gov/orgs/pa/newsbulletin/2004/04/23/text04.shtml |title=Physicist Thouless to give two talks at Lab |access-date=4 October 2016 |url-status=bot: unknown |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20061015000324/http://www.lanl.gov/orgs/pa/newsbulletin/2004/04/23/text04.shtml |archive-date=15 October 2006  }}, [[Los Alamos National Laboratory]]</ref> He was the winner of the 1990 [[Wolf Prize in Physics|Wolf Prize]] and a laureate of the 2016 Nobel Prize for physics along with [[Duncan Haldane|F. Duncan M. Haldane]] and [[J. Michael Kosterlitz]] for theoretical discoveries of [[topological phase]] transitions and topological phases of matter.<ref>{{Cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=Z4PN4GnsrSgC&q=%22Thouless,+david+james%22+1934|title=The international who's who 1991–92|date=25 July 1991|publisher=Europa Publ.|via=Google Books|isbn=9780946653706}}</ref>

==Education==
Born on 21 September 1934 in [[Bearsden]], Scotland <ref>{{cite news|last1=Sturrock|first1=Laura|title=Bearsden scientist is awarded Nobel prize in Physics|url=http://www.kirkintilloch-herald.co.uk/news/bearsden-scientist-is-awarded-nobel-prize-in-physics-1-4249500|access-date=6 October 2016|work=Kirkintilloch Herald|date=5 October 2016}}</ref> to English parents, Priscilla (Gorton) Thouless, an English teacher, and psychologist and broadcaster, [[Robert Thouless]], <ref>[https://www.nytimes.com/2019/04/22/obituaries/david-thouless-dead.html David Thouless, 84, Dies; Nobel Laureate Cast Light on Matter] New York Times, 2019-04-22.</ref> David Thouless was educated at [[Winchester College]] and earned a Bachelor of Arts degree in [[Natural Sciences (Cambridge)|Natural Sciences]] from the [[University of Cambridge]] as an undergraduate student of [[Trinity Hall, Cambridge]].<ref name=bbc/> He obtained his PhD at [[Cornell University]],<ref name=whoswho>{{Who's Who | surname = Thouless | othernames = Prof. David James | id = U37668 | volume = 2016 | edition = online [[Oxford University Press]]|location=Oxford}} {{subscription required}}</ref><ref name=tphd>{{cite thesis |degree=PhD |first=David James|last=Thouless |title=The application of perturbation methods to the theory of nuclear matter |publisher=Cornell University |year=1958 |url=https://catalog.hathitrust.org/Record/010004677 |oclc=745509629}}</ref> where [[Hans Bethe]] was his doctoral advisor.<ref name=mathgene/><ref>{{Cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=Ikrt42W9IOgC&pg=PA43|title=From Nuclei to Stars: Festschrift in Honor of Gerald E. Brown|first=Sabine|last=Lee|date=8 April 2011|publisher=World Scientific|via=Google Books|isbn=9789814329880}}</ref>

==Career and research==
Thouless was a [[postdoctoral researcher]] at [[Lawrence Berkeley Laboratory]], [[University of California, Berkeley]], and also worked in the physics department from 1958 to 1959, giving a course on atomic physics.<ref name="nobelfacts"/><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.washington.edu/news/2016/10/04/uw-professor-emeritus-david-j-thouless-wins-nobel-prize-in-physics-for-exploring-exotic-states-of-matter/|title=UW Professor Emeritus David J. Thouless wins Nobel Prize in physics for exploring exotic states of matter {{!}} UW Today|website=www.washington.edu|language=en|access-date=7 April 2017}}</ref><ref name="ACAP David Thouless">{{cite web|url=https://www.aip.org/history/acap/biographies/bio.jsp?thoulessd|title=David Thouless|publisher=aip.org|access-date=10 October 2016|url-status=dead|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20161005130847/https://www.aip.org/history/acap/biographies/bio.jsp?thoulessd|archive-date=5 October 2016}}</ref> He was the first director of studies in physics at [[Churchill College, Cambridge|Churchill College]], Cambridge, in 1961–1965, professor of mathematical physics at the [[University of Birmingham]] in the United Kingdom in 1965–1978,<ref name="UoBNobel">{{cite web|url=http://www.birmingham.ac.uk/news/latest/2016/10/former-birmingham-scientists-nobel-prize.aspx|title=Two former Birmingham scientists awarded Nobel Prize for Physics|date=4 October 2016|publisher=[[University of Birmingham]]|access-date=4 October 2016}}</ref> and professor of applied science at [[Yale University]] from 1979 to 1980,<ref name="ACAP David Thouless"/> before becoming a professor of physics at the [[University of Washington]]<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Nijs|first=Marcel den|date=2019-05-31|title=David Thouless (1934–2019)|journal=Science|language=en|volume=364|issue=6443|pages=835|doi=10.1126/science.aax9125|issn=0036-8075|pmid=31147511|s2cid=206668153}}</ref> in [[Seattle]] in 1980.<ref name="UoBNobel" /> Thouless made many theoretical contributions to the understanding of extended systems of atoms and electrons, and of nucleons.<ref name=Winner>{{Cite web|url=https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/physics/2016/summary/|title=The Nobel Prize in Physics 2016|website=NobelPrize.org}}</ref><ref name=naturenob>{{cite journal|last1=Gibney|first1=Elizabeth|last2=Castelvecchi|first2=Davide|title=Physics of 2D exotic matter wins Nobel: British-born theorists recognized for work on topological phases|journal=Nature|volume=538|issue=7623|year=2016|pages=18|publisher=[[Springer Nature]]|location=London|doi=10.1038/nature.2016.20722|pmid=27708331|bibcode=2016Natur.538...18G|doi-access=free}}</ref><ref name=nobelfacts/> He also worked on [[superconductivity]] phenomena, properties of nuclear matter, and excited collective motions within nuclei.<ref name=Winner/><ref name=naturenob/><ref name=nobelfacts/>

Thouless made many important contributions to the theory of [[many-body problem]]s.<ref name=nobelfacts>{{cite web|url=https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/physics/2016/thouless/facts/|title=David J. Thouless Facts|publisher=Nobel Prize.org|access-date=October 13, 2020}}</ref> For [[Atomic nucleus|atomic nuclei]], he cleared up the concept of 'rearrangement energy' and derived an expression for the [[moment of inertia]] of deformed nuclei.<ref name=nobelfacts/> In [[statistical mechanics]], he contributed many ideas to the understanding of ordering, including the concept of '[[topological ordering]]'.<ref name=nobelfacts/> Other important results relate to localised [[Electron configuration|electron states]] in disordered [[Crystal lattice|lattice]]s.<ref name=frs/><ref name=nobelfacts/>

==Personal life==
Thouless married Margaret Elizabeth Scrase in 1958 and together they had three children.<ref name=bbc/> In 2016, Thouless was reported to be suffering from [[dementia]].<ref>{{cite news|last1=Knapton|first1=Sarah|title=British scientists win Nobel prize in physics for work so baffling it had to be described using bagels|url=https://www.telegraph.co.uk/science/2016/10/04/british-scientists-win-nobel-prize-in-physics-for-work-so-baffli/|newspaper=The Telegraph|date=4 October 2016|access-date=24 September 2017}}</ref> He died on 6 April 2019 in Cambridge, aged 84.<ref name="Obit">{{cite web|url=https://www.trinhall.cam.ac.uk/news/professor-david-thouless-1934-2019/|title=Professor David Thouless 1934–2019|date=6 April 2019|publisher=[[Trinity Hall, Cambridge]]|access-date=8 April 2019}}</ref>
